Indian Agriculture (1950-1990)

1. Pre-Green Revolution Period (1950-1965)

Low Productivity & Traditional Methods: Agriculture was largely dependent on rainfall, and traditional farming methods were prevalent.
Food Shortages & Dependence on Imports: India faced severe food shortages and relied on imports, mainly from the U.S. under the PL-480 program.

Land Reforms:

  • Abolition of Zamindari system
  • Tenancy reforms
  • Land ceiling acts

However, implementation was weak in many regions.

Community Development Programs (CDP) & Five-Year Plans:

  • First Five-Year Plan (1951-56): Focused on agriculture and irrigation.
  • Introduction of cooperative farming and Integrated Rural Development programs.
  • Promotion of improved seeds, chemical fertilizers, and irrigation facilities.

Institutional Support:

  • Reserve Bank of India (RBI) played a key role in financing agriculture.
  • NABARD (established later in 1982) also supported agricultural development.

2. Post-Green Revolution Period (1965-1990)

Green Revolution (1965-1985):

  • Introduction of High Yielding Variety (HYV) seeds, chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and modern irrigation techniques.
  • Focused on wheat and rice production, especially in Punjab, Haryana, and western Uttar Pradesh.
  • Significant increase in food grain production, reducing India's dependence on imports.

Government Initiatives:

  • Minimum Support Price (MSP): Ensured stable income for farmers.
  • Public Distribution System (PDS): Provided food security.
  • Irrigation Projects: Expansion of canal irrigation and construction of major dams like Bhakra Nangal.
  • Agricultural Research & Institutions: Establishment of Indian Council of Agricultural Research (ICAR) and state agricultural universities.

Impact of Green Revolution:

  • Positive: Increased food grain production, self-sufficiency, rural employment.
  • Negative: Regional disparities, soil degradation, water depletion, and excessive use of chemicals leading to environmental concerns.

Conclusion

Between 1950-1990, Indian agriculture transformed from a subsistence-based economy to a more productive and semi-commercialized sector. While the Green Revolution helped in achieving food security, it also created new challenges like environmental degradation and unequal benefits among farmers.

Agriculture Sector in India (1950-1990)

1. Pre-Green Revolution Period (1950-1965)

Low Productivity & Traditional Methods: Agriculture relied heavily on rainfall, and traditional farming methods were prevalent.

Food Shortages & Dependence on Imports: Severe food shortages led to reliance on imports, mainly under the PL-480 program.

Land Reforms: The government introduced land reforms, but weak implementation limited success.

Community Development & Five-Year Plans: The First Five-Year Plan (1951-56) focused on irrigation, cooperative farming, and rural development.

Institutional Support: The RBI and later NABARD (1982) played key roles in financing agriculture.

2. Post-Green Revolution Period (1965-1990)

Green Revolution (1965-1985): HYV seeds, fertilizers, pesticides, and modern irrigation increased wheat and rice production, reducing import dependence.

Government Initiatives: MSP ensured farmer income stability, PDS provided food security, and major irrigation projects like Bhakra Nangal were launched.

Agricultural Research: ICAR and agricultural universities boosted research.

Impact: Increased food production, self-sufficiency, and employment. However, regional disparities, soil degradation, and excessive chemical use led to environmental concerns.

Conclusion

Between 1950-1990, Indian agriculture transformed into a more productive sector. The Green Revolution improved food security but introduced environmental and social challenges.

Industrial Sector in India (1950-1990)

1. Nehruvian Industrialization (1950-1965)

State-led Industrialization: Government played a key role under a socialist model.

Five-Year Plans: The Second Five-Year Plan (1956-61) emphasized heavy industries (steel, coal, machinery).

Industrial Policy Resolution, 1956: Industries were divided into state-owned, joint sector, and private sector.

Foreign Exchange Control: Import restrictions promoted indigenous industries (Import Substitution Industrialization).

2. License Raj & State Control (1965-1980)

License-Permit Raj: Businesses needed government approval for industrial activities.

Public Sector Expansion: Government-controlled major industries like banking, oil, and transport.

Impact: Public sector growth but inefficiency, corruption, and low private sector productivity.

3. Liberalization Attempts (1980-1990)

Economic Crisis & Fiscal Deficit: Public sector inefficiencies and government overspending created fiscal deficits.

New Industrial Policy (1985): Encouraged private participation and modernization.

Impact: Growth in consumer goods and early signs of liberalization, but heavy regulations persisted.

Conclusion

From 1950-1990, industrial policies were state-controlled, limiting private growth. Early liberalization in the 1980s set the stage for the 1991 reforms.

1991 Economic Crisis & LPG Reforms

Reasons for Economic Crisis

  • Balance of Payments Crisis: Forex reserves dropped below $1 billion, and India faced trade deficits.
  • High Fiscal Deficit: Government borrowing led to an 8.4% fiscal deficit.
  • Rising Inflation: Inflation soared over 13%, affecting essentials.
  • Public Sector Inefficiency: Loss-making PSUs burdened the economy.
  • Global Pressure: IMF bailout conditions forced India to open its economy.

1. Liberalization

Key Reforms: End of License Raj, FDI encouragement, import tariff reduction.

Impact: Increased competition, private sector growth, and foreign investments.

2. Privatization

Key Reforms: Disinvestment of PSUs, increased private sector participation, subsidy reductions.

Impact: Higher efficiency, reduced government burden, but job losses in PSUs.

3. Globalization

Key Reforms: Trade policy changes, FDI inflows, WTO membership (1995).

Impact: Economic growth, IT sector boom, and better product quality.

Overall Impact

  • Positive: Higher GDP, IT boom, job creation, infrastructure growth.
  • Negative: Income inequality, PSU job losses, inflation, environmental issues.

Conclusion

The 1991 LPG reforms transformed India into a global economic power, but challenges like inequality and environmental concerns remain.
